My professional identity

I enjoy finding solutions to problems and implementing ideas through clever design. This is something any engineer might say. What a typical engineer might neglect though, is to think about ways to incorporate sleek looks into a design. This is where I place myself as a designer. I am interested in blending robust and effective engineering solutions with eye-pleasing aesthetics. For this reason, I see the expertise areas of Technology & Realisation and Creativity & Aesthetics to be the most fascinating and valuable for my future career. 

As outlined in the section describing my past, the activities I undertook in high school allowed me to pick up the harder engineering skills such as modelling in CAD and understanding how to use software to create and execute a design with robust construction that fulfils specific functions. The courses of the first year of ID, on the other hand, complemented this knowledge by teaching me about the importance of the design process, the cycle of analysing, ideating, developing, and executing a design. I find myself now much more confident in being able to evaluate the feasibility of a design as well as be able to account for the needs and desires of consumers and/or society as a whole. 

While I am already competent in T&R and some aspects of C&A, which I was able to verify during the first-year projects, I discovered about myself a new desire to learn about entrepreneurship and the business aspect of design. My dream is to start a company that brings technological innovation to the market. However, I do not have much formal education in the area, with the Economics course in high school being the only notable example. Therefore, I want to support my curriculum with courses focused on the expertise area Business and Entrepreneurship.

Christian von Koenigsegg inside Koenigsegg Gemera (Koenigsegg 2019)

For this reason, one of my biggest role models is Christian von Koenigsegg, a Swedish founder and CEO, who was able to breach the difficult-to-enter automotive industry in 1994 with his company Koenigsegg, bringing excellent design and a number of innovations to the industry. This includes the introduction of carbon fibre chassis, a unique way of opening the doors of his cars or the Triplex suspension system, to name a few. What inspires me about Koenigsegg is that he is not just a CEO but also the person responsible for many of the patented innovations of his company and is actively involved in the design of his vehicles. I would like to breach the industry (not necessarily automotive) in a similar way one day.

My vision

I look forward to a bright future for humanity, but I expect that the current world situation will create new challenges that will demand us to change the way we live as a society. 

Firstly, with rising energy prices, people are more energy-conscious now than ever before. I see this personally with my family, who invested in solar panels and started to find many creative ways to reduce electricity consumption. This societal trend will create a demand for energy-efficient designs and will shape the direction products and systems are designed in the nearest future. Designers who do not take this into account will likely see their projects fall short.

Secondly, the world's population is growing rapidly in some areas and ageing in others. This leads to an increased demand for food in developing countries and fewer available workers in more developed ones. I believe the solution to the former problem is a better use of farming space through building vertical farms that utilize hydroponics. The latter problem can be addressed with robotics. These two technologies can be combined to create sustainable and efficient farming that is not labour intensive. Given that I involved myself in the robotics club in high school, and like to grow plants as a hobby, I can see myself exploring this direction as my possible future focus.
 
In terms of industrial design itself, I see CAD (Computer-aided design) becoming an even more prominent technology. Even though CAD has been a key part of industrial design for decades, I see CAD software that uses cloud technology as the future, since it makes design collaboration across distance much faster and easier. That is why I still lean towards using cloud-based Autodesk Fusion 360 rather than the much more established and more capable SolidWorks.

Another area I find fascinating and see a lot of potential in is additive manufacturing, as 3D printing has been my interest since I was 15. While the technology is ideal and well-used from the prototyping standpoint, I believe it will also gain prominence outside of prototyping, especially with the AI design capabilities now available. One of the main reasons for this is that I see the trend of personalisation of products becoming more important. Brands will be able to appeal to their customers’ specific wants and needs without drastically raising the costs. This can be a custom shape or pattern on a product or a more complex item such as a prosthetic limb or custom-fit wearable gear, such as skiing shoes. With additive manufacturing, it is possible to adjust the product to perfectly fit the customer’s body or preferences.
